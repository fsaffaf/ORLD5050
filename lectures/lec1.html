Overview: Ethical Evidence-Based Decision Making

Understanding how structured reasoning improves decisions under uncertainty.

Overview

Every day, we make many decisions. Some are simple, like deciding when to wake up on a Saturday. Others are complex, such as choosing how to invest money or make an important work decision.

Although we make many decisions every day, we rarely examine the decision-making process itself. Paying attention to that process helps us make better choices over time.

This course teaches a structured approach to decision-making using both ethics and evidence. The goal is not rigid rules, but thoughtful judgment under uncertainty.

What Is EEBDM?

EEBDM: A decision-making framework that integrates evidence, ethics, situational context, and practical constraints. It supports decisions that are reasonable, defensible, and adaptable.

EEBDM is a way of thinking about how decisions are made and how they can be improved. It combines evidence, ethics, and reflection into a single structured approach.

Decisions

Decisions involve choosing one action among multiple options, each with consequences. These decisions vary in importance, context, and risk level.

In real-world settings, it is rarely possible to evaluate every option completely. Instead, we often make satisficing decisions.

Satisficing: Choosing an option that is “good enough” given the constraints of time, information, and resources.

For example, a hospital administrator selecting software cannot evaluate every product in detail. They instead focus on key criteria such as reliability, privacy, and usability. This is not laziness — it is a rational strategy under constraints.

Satisficing helps decision-makers focus on what matters most in context, balancing evidence, ethics, and real-world limitations.

Evidence

Evidence: Information used to guide decisions, including data, expert opinion, experience, and prediction.

Decision-making is not about certainty. Most real-world decisions occur under uncertainty.

The key question is not whether something is proven with absolute certainty, but whether it is reasonable given the available evidence and context.

Different decisions require different levels of evidence rigor. Low-stakes decisions require less scrutiny than high-impact decisions.

Intuition also functions as a form of internal evidence, developed through experience and pattern recognition.

Knowledge does not come from a single study or source. It emerges over time through accumulation and comparison of evidence.

Ethics

Ethical values: Principles that define what is right, fair, and responsible.

A decision supported by strong evidence can still be ethically unacceptable. Therefore, ethical reasoning is a necessary component of EEBDM.

Ethics can be applied before decisions (eliminating unacceptable options) and after decisions (evaluating consequences and impact).

Ethical dilemmas occur when values conflict. Structured ethical frameworks help clarify and evaluate these conflicts systematically.

Key Takeaways

  • Decision-making is structured, not random or purely intuitive
  • Most real decisions involve satisficing under constraints
  • Evidence is about adequacy, not absolute certainty
  • Ethics is independent of evidence and must be evaluated separately
  • Good decisions integrate evidence, ethics, and context
