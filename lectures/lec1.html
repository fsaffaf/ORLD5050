Overview: Ethical Evidence-Based Decision Making

Understanding how structured reasoning improves decisions under uncertainty.

Overview

Every day, we make many decisions. Some are simple, like deciding when to wake up on a Saturday. Others are complex, such as choosing how to invest money or make an important work decision.

Although we make many decisions every day, we rarely examine the decision-making process itself. Paying attention to that process helps us make better choices over time.

This course teaches a structured approach to decision-making using both ethics and evidence. The goal is not rigid rules, but thoughtful judgment under uncertainty.

What Is EEBDM?

EEBDM: A decision-making framework that integrates evidence, ethics, situational context, and practical constraints.

EEBDM combines evidence, ethics, and reflection into a structured reasoning system.

Decisions

Decisions involve choosing one action among multiple options, each with consequences.

In real-world settings, we often use satisficing.

Satisficing: Choosing an option that is “good enough” given constraints of time, information, and resources.

Evidence

Evidence: Information used to guide decisions, including data, expertise, and experience.

Decisions are not about certainty, but adequacy under uncertainty.

Ethics

Ethical values: Principles defining what is right, fair, and responsible.

Evidence alone is not enough — ethical evaluation is required.

Key Takeaways

  • Decisions are structured, not random
  • Satisficing is a rational strategy
  • Evidence is about adequacy, not certainty
  • Ethics must be evaluated independently
  • Good decisions integrate all three
