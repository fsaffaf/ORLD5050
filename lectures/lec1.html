What is Ethical Evidence-Based Decision-Making?

Overview

Every day, we make many decisions. Some are simple, like deciding when to wake up on a Saturday. Others are complex, such as choosing how to invest money or make an important work decision.

Although we make many decisions daily, we rarely examine the decision-making process itself. Paying attention to that process improves decision quality over time.

This course introduces a structured approach using ethics and evidence to support decision-making under uncertainty.

Key Idea

Good decision-making is not random. It involves a structured process of identifying options, evaluating evidence, considering ethical implications, and choosing an action that fits the context.

What Is EEBDM?

EEBDM stands for Ethical Evidence-Based Decision Making.

EEBDM is a framework that integrates evidence, ethics, context, and constraints into decision-making.

It supports reasoning that is adaptive, defensible, and appropriate under uncertainty.

EEBDM

EEBDM helps decision-makers combine what they know, what they value, and what the situation requires. It is especially useful when decisions must be made under uncertainty.

Decisions

Decisions involve choosing between multiple actions with different possible consequences.

In real-world settings, we often use satisficing.

Satisficing means choosing an option that is “good enough” given constraints like time, information, and resources.

Satisficing

Satisficing is not careless decision-making. It is a practical and rational strategy when decision-makers face limited time, incomplete information, or competing priorities.

Evidence

Evidence includes data, experience, expert judgment, and prediction.

Decision-making is not about certainty, but about adequacy under uncertainty.

Evidence in Decision-Making

Evidence helps us judge what is likely, what is known, what remains uncertain, and what kinds of action are reasonable based on available information.

Ethics

Ethical values define what is right, fair, and responsible.

Evidence alone is not sufficient. Ethical evaluation is required for all meaningful decisions.

Ethical Evaluation

A decision may be supported by evidence but still raise ethical concerns. Ethical decision-making asks whether an action is fair, responsible, and justifiable.

Key Takeaways

  • Decision-making is structured, not random.
  • Satisficing is a rational strategy under constraints.
  • Evidence is about adequacy, not certainty.
  • Ethics must be evaluated independently of evidence.
  • Good decisions integrate evidence, ethics, and context.
