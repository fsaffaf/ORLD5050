Overview

Every day, we make many decisions. Some are simple, like choosing when to wake up on a Saturday. Others are complex, like investing money or making high-stakes work decisions.

We rarely examine the process of decision-making itself. Yet understanding that process is what improves outcomes over time.

This course develops a structured approach to decision-making using ethics and evidence. The goal is not rigid rules, but adaptive judgment under uncertainty.

What Is EEBDM?

EEBDM is a decision framework that integrates evidence, ethics, situational constraints, and practical limits. It supports reasoning that is defensible, context-sensitive, and adaptable.

Decisions

Decisions involve selecting one option among alternatives, each with consequences. Some are routine, others are strategic or high-risk.

In real-world conditions, full evaluation is rarely possible. Instead, we use satisficing decisions.

Satisficing: selecting an option that is “good enough” given constraints, rather than attempting to optimize perfectly.

For example, selecting hospital software requires prioritizing key criteria such as reliability, privacy, and usability rather than evaluating every feature. This is not shortcut thinking—it is structured constraint reasoning.

Evidence

Evidence: information used to guide decisions, including data, expertise, experience, and forecasts.

Evidence is not about certainty. Most decisions occur under uncertainty. The question is whether the evidence is sufficient and appropriate—not whether it is absolute.

Different decisions require different levels of evidence rigor. High-stakes decisions demand stronger validation than low-cost choices.

Intuition is also a form of evidence, derived from experience and pattern recognition. Effective reasoning often begins with intuition and expands when necessary.

No single study establishes absolute truth. Knowledge emerges cumulatively across multiple sources of evidence.

Ethics

Ethical values: principles guiding what is fair, right, and responsible.

A decision supported by evidence can still be unethical. EEBDM requires ethical evaluation alongside analytical reasoning.

Ethics can operate before decisions (filtering options) and after decisions (evaluating outcomes).

Ethical dilemmas arise when values conflict. Structured frameworks help evaluate competing principles systematically.
