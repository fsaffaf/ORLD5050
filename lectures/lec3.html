Overview

Constructing a clear and well-supported argument is central to Ethical, Evidence-Based Decision Making (EEBDM). In academic and professional contexts, arguments are structured forms of reasoning used to justify decisions.

In everyday language, “argument” often means disagreement. In academic contexts, however, it refers to a structured set of reasons supporting a conclusion.

Argument: An organized set of reasons that provide support for a specific decision or conclusion.

Basic Argument

Basic Argument: A structure consisting of a claim and one or more premises.
Term Definition Example
Claim The main point being argued. Single-use plastics should be banned.
Premise Reasons supporting the claim. Single-use plastics cause environmental harm.
Evidence Data supporting the premises. Plastic waste harms marine ecosystems.

Complex Argument

Complex Argument: A Toulmin-style argument including claim, premise, evidence, warrant, backing, qualifier, counterargument, and rebuttal.
Component Definition Example
Claim Main position being defended. Single-use plastics should be banned.
Premise Reason supporting the claim. They cause environmental harm.
Evidence Supporting data or facts. Ocean plastic pollution data.
Warrant Links premise to claim. Harmful products should be regulated or banned.
Backing Supports the warrant. Scientific research on plastic pollution.
Qualifier Limits scope of claim. Should be banned in most applicable cases.
Counterargument Opposing viewpoint. Some sectors rely on plastics for safety.
Rebuttal Response to counterargument. Exemptions and innovation can address limitations.

Key Takeaways

  • Arguments structure reasoning in decision making.
  • Basic arguments contain claims and premises.
  • Complex arguments include additional logical components.
  • Strong arguments depend on evidence and clear reasoning.
