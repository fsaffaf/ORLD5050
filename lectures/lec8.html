Overview

Making complex decisions can be hard, but EEBDM is designed to help you break down complex decisions into manageable pieces. When we break a difficult decision into smaller steps, it becomes easier to look at the facts, think about what is right or wrong, and choose an answer that makes sense for the situation.

 

Step 1: Gather Detailed Background Information

Gather information from each of the four sources of evidence. Identify what you know about the current situation, as well as examples of similar situations that have occured before (inside or outside of the organization). Do not feel pressured to gather every little bit of data there is on the topic. For most decisions, a small number of high-impact factors typically drive most of the outcome. Instead of trying to account for every possible influence, effective decision making often means focusing on the few elements that matter most.

Step 2: Identify High Quality Evidence

Evaluate the credibility of the evidence you gathered. Only keep evidence that appears to be high quality. Avoid using evidence that is low quality (as this evidence can be easily dismissed in counterarguments). Any evidence you use that has lower quality should be evidence you can defend in the case of a counterargument.

The CRAAP test is a useful and quick tool to determine information quality.

Step 3: Make a Decision Based on the Evidence

Once you have enough information and clear criteria, the next step is to look the decisions you could make. Typically, there are more than just two options (to do something or not do something). What are all possible options? Which decision is best supported by the evidence?

 

Step 4: Ensure the Decision is Ethically Defensible

Ensure your decision is supported by an ethically defensible perspective. It does not need to be supported by all perspectives, but it needs to be a justifiable perspective. Prepare rebutals to any counterarguments.

Step 6: Address any Limitations/ Conflicts

Think about what might weaken your decision or make it harder to carry out. This could include missing information, disagreements between people involved, limited time or resources, or possible unintended consequences. Be honest about what you don’t know and how that uncertainty might affect your choice.

Step 7: Review for Any Bias / Heuristics

Go back and check your thinking for bias or shortcuts. Ask yourself if you only focused on information that supported your idea, ignored important opposing views, or made assumptions too quickly. Try to spot anything that may have influenced your decision unfairly or without enough evidence.
