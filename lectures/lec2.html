Overview

Decision making involves two closely connected types of thinking: intuitive thinking and analytical thinking.

Intuitive Thinking

A fast, automatic form of thinking based on patterns, experience, and immediate impressions.

Analytical Thinking

A slower, more deliberate form of thinking that involves carefully evaluating information, evidence, and possible outcomes.

In many Western countries, such as the United States, Australia, and England, decision making tends to favor analytical reasoning over intuitive thinking.

In contrast, Eastern countries such as China, India, and Japan have largely preserved the role of intuition alongside analytical thought.

Research suggests that these processes are not separate but work together during decision making. For example, when searching for a house, intuition may draw your attention to certain listings that feel appealing, while analytical reasoning helps you review details such as price, inspections, and long-term suitability.

Intuition helps narrow options quickly, while analytical thinking helps evaluate them more thoroughly. Effective decision making therefore comes from balancing intuitive judgments with careful analysis and knowing when each approach should guide a choice.

Heuristics

The human brain cannot process every piece of information available all the time, every day. If it did, it would take too long to make even small everyday decisions, such as when to wake up, what to eat, what to wear, or how to get to class.

Therefore, the human brain often uses mental shortcuts called heuristics.

Heuristics

Heuristics are intentional, analytical mental shortcuts that allow us to make faster decisions. The brain automatically focuses on the most important information and ignores other details. Decisions made using heuristics can be accurate or inaccurate.

The Benefits

These shortcuts allow us to make practical decisions in complex situations. They are widely used in everyday life and help us function efficiently in environments where time, information, and cognitive resources are limited.

For example, if a decision made yesterday worked successfully, such as leaving the house at a particular time to arrive at class on time, it may make sense to make the same choice today. However, decisions made using heuristics are not guaranteed to be accurate.

The Limitations

Heuristics focus on only a small portion of the available information. If important details are ignored, the decision may be flawed.

For example, choosing a job candidate based only on GPA may overlook important qualities such as work experience. Additionally, heuristics typically work best in environments where we have relevant experience. When situations are new or unfamiliar, the shortcut may no longer be reliable.

For example, brand recognition may help when buying common products but may fail when choosing items in a completely new category.

The Solution

Our brain will always use heuristics, whether we want it to or not. Therefore, in EEBDM, we analyze our heuristics to determine when they are useful and when they are not useful.

To analyze our heuristics, we must become more attentive to the criteria driving our decisions: what we focus on, what we overlook, and why. When those criteria are thoughtfully chosen, even simplified decisions can be well-reasoned.

Heuristics help people make fast, efficient, and often effective decisions. However, because they rely on simplified information, they can sometimes produce biases or mistakes. Effective decision making involves recognizing when heuristics are useful and when more careful analysis is needed.

Examples of Heuristics

Term Definition Helpful Harmful
Well-Known Heuristic When choosing between two options, you pick the one you recognize. This can be helpful when information is limited, costly to obtain, or when speed is more important than accuracy. It can be harmful when recognition is influenced by advertising or repetition rather than quality. Choosing a familiar brand of laptop in a store when you need one quickly and cannot research all options. The product may be well-known because it has been successful over time. Choosing a well-known brand of medicine just because you recognize it from advertisements, even though a better option is available.
Popularity Heuristic Choosing the option that most people choose. This can be helpful when the group has good information and experience. It can be harmful when the group is uninformed or following a trend. Using a study app that is popular because it is effective at helping students learn information. Choosing a restaurant just because it is popular on social media, even though the popularity may be driven by paid influencer promotion rather than food quality.
Similarity Heuristic Choosing an option that is similar to something that worked before. This is helpful when situations are truly similar and past experience is reliable. It is harmful when situations are different but assumed to be the same. Using the same revision strategy that worked well for a similar exam format. Hiring a job candidate because they feel similar to a previous successful employee, even though the job requirements are different.
Auto-Pilot Heuristic Accepting the default option without actively evaluating alternatives. This is helpful when the default is designed by experts and is a safe, efficient choice. It is harmful when the default is not in your best interest. Keeping the default payment method in a trusted app because it is secure and convenient. Not changing default privacy settings that share more personal data than intended.
Emotion Heuristic Making decisions based on feelings instead of analysis. This is helpful when emotions reflect real experience or warning signals. It is harmful when emotions are caused by stress, anger, or temporary mood. Avoiding a deal because something feels unsafe based on past negative experience. Quitting a job immediately after getting angry in a meeting, then regretting it later.
Recent Heuristic Assuming recent events are likely to happen again. This is helpful when recent changes reflect a real trend. It is harmful when recent events are rare or misleading. Choosing an alternative route after noticing a recent rise in traffic accidents due to construction. Hearing about a car accident in the news and then assuming you are likely to get into one soon.

Biases

Biases are related to heuristics. Heuristics are often within our conscious awareness, while biases are often outside of our conscious awareness. In other words, we often know when we are using a heuristic, but we may not know when a bias is influencing us.

You can think of biases as intuitive heuristics. Biases influence how we interpret information and make decisions, usually outside of conscious awareness. Like heuristics, biases allow us to make quick decisions when time or information is limited.

Biases

Biases are unintentional, intuitive mental shortcuts that allow us to make faster decisions. The brain automatically focuses on certain information and ignores other details. Decisions made using biases can be accurate or inaccurate.

Benefits

Biases can provide practical advantages by helping individuals and groups make faster, more efficient decisions. For example, filtering and prioritizing information can help focus attention when someone feels overwhelmed.

A doctor may focus on the most likely diagnosis rather than investigating every rare possibility. Anchoring decisions in core beliefs can also offer consistency and stability by helping choices align with long-held values.

Biases can also support value-driven choices, such as when a nonprofit leader favors projects aligned with the organization’s mission. In uncertain situations, biases may help people act quickly by highlighting relevant cues, such as an emergency responder prioritizing victims showing visible distress.

Finally, some biases reinforce identity and social cohesion, promote harmony within groups, and reduce cognitive dissonance by helping individuals maintain a sense of self-coherence.

Limitations

While adaptive, biases can also create significant problems. Filtering and prioritizing information can lead to overlooking critical evidence, resulting in one-sided decisions.

Anchoring in core beliefs may block necessary change, causing stagnation or missed opportunities. Value-driven simplification can prevent flexible thinking and limit better outcomes.

Biases that help people act under uncertainty may also cause hidden issues to be ignored in favor of visible or familiar problems. Biases that reinforce group cohesion can lead to groupthink and exclude diverse perspectives.

Reducing cognitive dissonance may block honest reflection, while preserving core worldviews can prevent people from incorporating new evidence that would improve decision quality.

Solutions

To manage the drawbacks of biases while preserving their benefits, decision makers should actively evaluate assumptions, seek diverse perspectives, and test beliefs against evidence.

Techniques such as structured reflection, checklists, and pre-mortem exercises can make biases visible and adjustable. Encouraging flexibility, questioning entrenched views, and treating beliefs as probabilistic rather than absolute can reduce the risk of overcommitment to flawed judgments while retaining the efficiency and coherence that biases provide.

Examples of Biases

Term Definition Helpful Harmful
Confirmation Bias Focusing on information that supports your pre-existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. Helpful for experts quickly filtering information in familiar situations. Harmful when trying to understand new or complex situations.
Sunk Cost Bias Continuing to invest in a decision because of prior investments. Helpful when it is reasonably predicted that the investment will pay off. Harmful when it is predicted that the investment will not pay off.
Overconfidence Bias Overestimating one’s skills, abilities, or judgment. Helpful when it encourages quick action, especially in competitive environments where reasonable risk-taking can create opportunities. Harmful when it leads to underestimating risks or failing to create backup plans.
Anchoring Bias Focusing too much on the first piece of information encountered, also known as the anchor, when making decisions. Helpful when the anchor is legitimate because it provides a starting point in ambiguous situations. Harmful when the anchor is irrelevant or misleading and prevents proper adjustment when new information emerges.
Loyalty Bias Staying loyal to a group or organization. Helpful when it supports long-term team building and group stability. Harmful when it protects underperformance, unethical behavior, or poor decisions.

Key Takeaways

  • Decision making involves both intuitive and analytical thinking.
  • Heuristics are intentional shortcuts that help us make faster decisions.
  • Biases are often unconscious shortcuts that shape how we interpret information.
  • Both heuristics and biases can be helpful or harmful depending on the context.
  • Effective decision making requires knowing when to rely on shortcuts and when to slow down for analysis.
