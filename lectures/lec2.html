Overview

Decision making involves two closely connected types of thinking: intuitive thinking and analytical thinking.

Intuitive Thinking: A fast, automatic form of thinking based on patterns, experience, and immediate impressions. 
Analytical Thinking:  A slower, more deliberate thinking that involves carefully evaluating information, evidence, and possible outcomes.

In many Western countries, such as the USA, Australia, and England, decision tends to favor analytical reasoning over intuitive thinking.

In contrast, Eastern countries like China, India, and Japan have largely preserved the role of intuition alongside analytical thought.

Research suggests these processes are not separate but work together during decision making. For example, when searching for a house, intuition may draw your attention to certain listings that feel appealing, while analytical reasoning helps you review details such as price, inspections, and long-term suitability. Intuition helps narrow options quickly, while analytical thinking helps evaluate them more thoroughly.

Effective decision making therefore comes from balancing intuitive judgments with careful analysis and knowing when each approach should guide a choice.

 

Heuristics

The human brain cannot process every piece of information available, all the time, everyday. If we did, it would take forever to make every little decision in our everyday life (when to wakeup, what to eat, what to wear, how to get to class, etc). Therefore, the human brain often uses mental shortcuts called heuristics.

Heuristics: Intentional, analytical mental shortcuts that allow us to make faster decisions. The brain automatically focuses on the most important information and ignores the other details. The decisions made using heuristics can be accurate or inaccurate.

 

The Benefits
These shortcuts allow us to make practical decisions in complex situations. These shortcuts are widely used in everyday life and help us function efficiently in environments where time, information, and cognitive resources are limited. For example, if a decision we made yesterday worked successfully (like the decision of when to leave the house to arrive at class on time), why not make the same choice today (leave at the house the same time as yesterday)? However, it is not guaranteed that the decisions made using heuristics will always be accurate.

 

The Limitations
Heuristics focus on only a small portion of the available information. If important details are ignored, the decision may be flawed. For example, choosing a job candidate based only on GPA may overlook important qualities such as work experience. Additionally, heuristics typically work best in environments where we have relevant experience. When situations are new or unfamiliar, the shortcut may no longer be reliable. For example, brand recognition may help when buying common products but may fail when choosing items in a completely new category.

 

The Solution
Our brain will always use heuristics, whether we want to our not. Therefore, in EEBDM, we analyze our heuristics to determine when they are useful (lead to accurate decisions) versus not useful (lead to innacurate decisions). To analyze our heuristics, we must become more attentive to the criteria driving our decisions—what we focus on, what we overlook, and why. When those criteria are thoughtfully chosen, even simplified decisions can be well-reasoned ones. Heuristics help people make fast, efficient, and often effective decisions, but because they rely on simplified information, they can sometimes produce biases or mistakes. Effective decision making involves recognizing when heuristics are useful and when more careful analysis is needed.

You notice a recent rise in traffic accidents on a specific road due to construction reports, so you choose an alternative route. Biases

Biases are related to heuristics. Heuristics are often within our conscious awareness (we know when we are using them), while biases are often outside of our conscious awareness (we often do not know when we are using them). You can think of biases as intuitive heuristics. Biases influence how we interpret information and make decisions, usually outside of conscious awareness. Like heuristics, biases allow us to make quick decisions when time or information is limited. 

Biases: Unintentional, intuitive mental shortcuts that allow us to make faster decisions. The brain automatically focuses on the most important information and ignores the other details. The decisions made using biases can be accurate or inaccurate.

 

Benefits
Biases can provide practical advantages by helping individuals and groups make faster, more efficient decisions. For example, filtering and prioritizing information focuses attention on key aspects when overwhelmed, allowing a doctor to concentrate on the most likely diagnosis rather than investigating every rare possibility. Anchoring decisions in core beliefs offers consistency and stability, giving confidence that choices align with long-held values. Streamlining and enforcing value-driven choices simplifies decisions by emphasizing ethical or moral priorities, as seen when a nonprofit leader favors projects aligned with the organization’s mission. Facilitating decisions under uncertainty helps act quickly by highlighting relevant cues, like an emergency responder prioritizing victims showing visible distress. Finally, reinforcing identity and social cohesion promotes harmony within groups, supporting collective well-being, and reducing cognitive dissonance preserves self-coherence, helping individuals rationalize past choices.

 

Limitations
While adaptive, these biases can also create significant problems. Filtering and prioritizing information can lead to overlooking critical evidence, resulting in one-sided decisions. Anchoring in core beliefs may block necessary change, causing stagnation or missed opportunities. Value-driven simplification can prevent flexible thinking, limiting better outcomes. Facilitating decisions under uncertainty may cause hidden issues to be ignored in favor of visible or familiar problems. Biases that reinforce group cohesion can lead to groupthink, excluding diverse perspectives. Reducing cognitive dissonance may block honest reflection, while preserving core worldviews can prevent incorporating new evidence that would improve decision quality.

 

Solutions
To manage the drawbacks of biases while preserving their benefits, decision makers should actively evaluate assumptions, seek diverse perspectives, and test beliefs against evidence. Techniques such as structured reflection, checklists, and “pre-mortem” exercises can make biases visible and adjustable. Encouraging flexibility, questioning entrenched views, and treating beliefs as probabilistic rather than absolute can reduce the risk of overcommitment to flawed judgments while retaining the efficiency and coherence that biases provide.

 

Term Definition Helpful Harmful
Confirmation Bias When you focus on information that supports your pre-existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. Helpful for experts quickly filtering information in familiar situations.

Harmful when trying to understand new situations.
Sunk Cost Bias Continuing to invest in a decision due to prior investments. Helpful when it is predicted the investment will pay off. Harmful when it is predicted the investment will NOT pay off.
Overconfidence Bias

The overestimation of one's skills or abilities.

Helpful when encourages quick action, especially in competitive environments where risk taking leads to opportunities.

Harmful when leads to underestimation of risks and no back-up plans.
Anchoring Bias

Focusing too much on the first piece of information encountered (the “anchor”) when making decisions.

Helpful when the anchor is legitimate. It provides a starting point in ambiguous situations, speeding up judgment.

Harmful when the anchor is irrelevant or misleading, preventing proper adjustment when new information emerges
Loyalty Bias

Staying loyal to a group or organization.

Helpful when it leads to long-term team building and group stability.

Harmful when it protects underperformance, unethical behavior, or poor decisions.
