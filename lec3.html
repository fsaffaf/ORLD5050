Overview

Constructing a clear and well-supported argument is a central component of Ethical, Evidence-Based Decision Making (EEBDM). Whether in academic, professional, or everyday situations, individuals must often justify their decisions by providing reasons and evidence. Understanding how arguments are structured and how they can be evaluated allows decision makers to communicate their reasoning effectively and assess the reasoning of others.

In everyday language, the word "argument" often refers to a disagreement or conflict. In academic and professional contexts, however, an argument has a more precise meaning. 

Argument: In the professional world, an argument is an organized set of reasons that provide support for a specific decision. 

 

 

Basic Argument

Basic Argument: A specific type of argument. Basic arguments are made of two parts: the claim (or conclusion) and the premises.
Term Definition Example Statement
Claim The main point someone is trying to prove or convince others to accept.  Single-use plastics should be banned.
Premise The reasons to support the claim. They provide the justification for why the claim should be accepted as true or reasonable. Single-use plastics cause significant environmental harm.
Evidence The reasons (premises) to support a claim are typically backed by evidence. Plastic waste pollutes oceans and harms marine life.

 

 

Complex Argument

Complex Argument: Complex arguments (sometimes called Toulmin arguments), are made up of the claim, premises, warrants, backing, qualifiers, rebuttals, and counterarguments.
Component Definition Example Statement
Claim The main point someone is trying to convince others to accept.  Single-use plastics should be banned.
Premise The specific reason we should support the claim (the explanation of how the evidence applies to the claim). Single-use plastics cause significant environmental harm.
Evidence The general data/evidence that supports the premise. Plastic waste pollutes oceans and harms marine life.
Warrant The sentence that connects the premise and the claim together. Products that cause substantial, long-term environmental harm should be regulated or banned when safer, practical alternatives are available.
Backing Additional evidence that supports the warrant.
This is a good place to put your ethical perspective.		 Scientific research demonstrates that plastics are a leading source of ocean pollution, with millions of tons entering marine ecosystems annually. Policy interventions in countries such as France and Kenya have shown measurable reductions in plastic waste following bans or restrictions.
Qualifier A statement indicating the strength or scope of the claim, often expressed with terms such as most, usually, or likely. Single-use plastics should be banned in most cases where affordable and effective alternatives (e.g., reusable or biodegradable materials) are accessible.
Counterargument Opposing viewpoint that challenges the claim. I acknowledge that bans may create challenges in sectors where alternatives are not yet scalable, affordable, or equally effective (e.g., medical, food safety, or low-income contexts).
Rebuttal A response to a counterargument that defends the original claim. While single-use plastics do serve important functions in certain sectors, targeted exemptions (e.g., for medical use) and gradual policy implementation can preserve these benefits while still reducing overall plastic waste. Additionally, innovation and economies of scale can lower the cost of alternatives over time, mitigating concerns about affordability and access.
