Overview

A key piece of Ethical Evidence Based Decision Making (EEBDM) is ethics. Sometimes, there isn’t a clearly “right” choice. In these cases, we have to balance competing values to decide on a course of action that can be ethically defended. Combining ethical thinking with evidence-based reasoning helps us tackle real-world problems more effectively. It increases the chances that our decisions are both morally responsible and well-informed.

People often put their morals and personal values above ethics and facts. This is a common challenge in Ethical Evidence-Based Decision Making. When moral beliefs clash with evidence, people might ignore, twist, or reinterpret the evidence to make choices that fit their ethical views. Understanding this tension is crucial for making good decisions when both ethics and facts matter.

People can have very different ideas of what a morally desirable outcome is. These ideas are shaped by culture, society, or personal values, which can lead to different judgments about what actions are ethical—even when everyone is looking at the same facts.

Term Definition
Ethics The study of moral principles and how they apply to behavior. Often formalized in philosophies, professional codes, or frameworks used to evaluate right and wrong.
Ethical Perspective A set of moral principles that have been made into a formal philosophy or professional code to evaluate right and wrong. There are six main ethical perspectives.
Morals An individual’s or group’s informal beliefs about what is right and wrong. Morals are influenced by culture, religion, upbringing, and personal values.
Personal Values What matters to us individually, including our preferences, goals, and sources of satisfaction. These values often drive hedonic motivations.
Hedonic Motivation The natural human tendency to seek pleasure and avoid pain or discomfort, which can influence choices and behavior.
Ethical Satisficing Making decisions that are both based on ethics and evidence with our limited time, attention, or information. It means being aware of our biases, understanding how emotions and situations can shape what we focus on, and intentionally reminding ourselves to consider how our choices affect others when that focus starts to slip.

 

The Six Ethical Perspectives

Ethical Perspective Question Main Focus
People's Rights Does this decision respect everyone’s basic rights? Safety, freedom, privacy, consent.
Fairness Is this decision fair to everyone involved? Equal treatment, justice, what people deserve.
Consequences Will this decision lead to the best overall outcome? Benefits vs. harms
Community Well-Being How does this decision affect the group/ community? Shared resources, social trust, common good.
Character Does this decision reflect who I want to be? Honesty, courage, integrity, moral consistency.
Relationships Does this decision consider others’ needs/ feelings? Empathy, kindness, maintaining bonds.

 

Steps for Making an Ethical Decision

  1. Identify the ethical issue.
  2. Gather all relevant and reliable information, considering both the immediate and long-term consequences.
  3. Determine what decision you should make based on each of the six ethical perspectives.
  4. Select the decision that is most ethically defensible and supported by the evidence.
