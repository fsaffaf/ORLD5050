Overview

Every day, we make many decisions. Some are simple, like deciding when to wakeup on Saturday. Other decisions are more complex, such as choosing how to invest money or make an important work decision.

Although we make many decisions every day, we do not often think about the process of making a decision. Paying attention to the decision process itself helps us make better choices.

This class will teach you the best process for making decisions by using both ethics and evidence. By integrating evidence, ethics, and reflection, EEBDM helps decision makers make choices that are reasonable, defensible, and adaptable. Rather than following rigid rules, EEBDM encourages thoughtful, context-sensitive judgment under uncertainty.

What Is EEBDM?

EEBDM: An method to make good decisions. It combines evidence, ethics, situation, and practical limits. It is a way of thinking about how decisions are made and how decision processes can be improved. EEBDM combines three core elements: decisions, evidence, and ethics. 

Decisions

Decisions involve choosing one action among multiple options, each with consequences. Decisions vary in importance, context, and stakes. Some are routine, while others are strategic or high-risk.

In real life, it is rarely possible to evaluate all options perfectly. Instead of fully satisfying decisions, we often make satisficing decisions.

Satisficing Decisions: The option that is not perfect, but is "good enough" for the situation.

For example, a hospital administrator choosing new software cannot evaluate every product in detail. Instead, they focus on key criteria, such as reliability, privacy, and usability. Features that do not matter are ignored. This is not laziness. It is a realistic and ethical strategy. 

Satisficing helps decision makers focus on what matters most in context. It supports EEBDM by balancing evidence, ethics, and real-world limits.

Evidence

Evidence: Evidence is the information used to guide decisions. This can include data, expert opinions, past experience, or forecasts. Good evidence is not just available—it is relevant, credible, and appropriate for the decision.

Decision making is not about proof or certainty. Most real-world decisions involve uncertainty. The question is not “Is this 100% proven?” but “Is this reasonable given the evidence and the situation?”

Different decisions require different levels of confidence. Buying a low-cost item requires little evidence. Making a major financial or medical decision requires much more. Evidence standards should match risk and consequences.

EEBDM also recognizes intuition as a form of internal evidence. Intuition comes from experience and pattern recognition. An effective approach starts with intuition and expands evidence only when needed.

Finally, no single study "proves" anything as 100% fact. Knowledge develops over time through many studies. EEBDM avoids claims of proof and instead asks whether evidence is sufficient and appropriate.

Ethics

Ethical values: Moral principles about what is right, fair, and responsible.

EEBDM requires that decisions be not only effective but also ethical. A decision supported by evidence can still be unethical.

Ethics can be applied before a decision, by ruling out unacceptable options, and after a decision, by evaluating outcomes. Ethical reflection helps ensure decisions remain aligned with values over time.

Ethical dilemmas arise when values conflict. Tools like the Santa Clara University framework help decision makers examine options from multiple ethical perspectives. EEBDM encourages this kind of structured ethical reflection.
