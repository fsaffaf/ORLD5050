Overview

Making a decision requires evidence. However, evidence is not about quantity. It is about quality. You need to know what kind of evidence to use, when to use it, and why it matters. The sources, types, and quality of evidence can strongly shape the decisions we make. Evidence is not just information. It is a strategic and ethical resource that supports valid and defensible decisions. In EEBDM, the main challenge is usually not finding information but using it well. Different sources of evidence can be combined to support complex decisions. Tools such as AI can strengthen our ability to organize and analyze evidence, but they cannot fully replace human judgment. The quality of a decision depends on the evidence we select and how we interpret, evaluate, and apply it within the limits of a specific situation. 

Evidence: Any information that can influence decision making. This includes preferences, attitudes, prior knowledge, experience, empirical data, situational details, and anticipated costs and consequences tied to potential choices. 

 

The Four Sources of Evidence

There are four main places you can gather evidenec to support your arguments. All four sources are useful, and ideally you should use all four when presenting an argument.

SOURCE DEFINITION EXAMPLE
Expert Opinion

An expert individual's opinion on what decision should be made.

An expert is someone with the relevant degrees and experience.

A company is deciding whether to enter a new international market in Southeast Asia.

The company could ask the opinion of an experienced market entry consultant who specializes in the Southeast Asian market.
Stakeholder Opinion

A stakeholder's individual opinion on what decision should be made.

A stakeholder is someone who is personally affected by the decision. This includes investors, employees, and the community.

A company is deciding whether to enter a new international market in Southeast Asia.

The company could ask their investors what they want to do.
Internal Data

Formal data from INSIDE the organization of interest.

Formal data includes company financial reports, employee performance data, customer data, and website analytics.

A company is deciding whether to enter a new international market in Southeast Asia.

The company reviews their current financial reports to determine if they have the funds to invest in a new market.
External Data

Formal data that comes from OTHER organizations.

This includes market research reports, competitor data, news reports, and academic/scientific research.

A company is deciding whether to enter a new international market in Southeast Asia.

The company reviews their competitor's performance data to see if they are having success in Southeast Asia.

 

Credibility of Evidence (CRAAP Test)

Because there is an overwhelming volume of information available, assessing credibility is vital whether we’re evaluating scientific research, internal reports, stakeholder perspectives, or media sources.

Currency: Is this information up to date?
Information that is recent is generally more useful, especially in fast-changing fields like technology or current events.

Relevance: Does this information directly address my topic, question, or decision?
Information that closely addresses your topic or specific decision is best. High-quality evidence that does not speak to the central question are not useful. For instance, if you are researching U.S. laws, sources about European legal systems, no matter how detailed or credible, would not be directly relevant. 

Authority: Who is providing this information, and do they have the credentials or expertise to be credible?
The people who produced the information should have the proper credentials. For example, a psychiatrist with specialized training in mental health is an authoritative source for treating severe psychiatric disorders, while a general practitioner without relevant experience may lack sufficient authority on the same topic. Recognizing authority helps assess the credibility of the evidence source.

Accuracy: Is the content reliable and supported by other credible sources?
Information must be supported by verifiable evidence, such as peer-reviewed studies, citations, or reproducible data. It also involves checking for consistency across multiple sources to ensure that the conclusions are reliable. Small discrepancies between sources are normal and often reflect nuances, but unsupported claims or selective reporting raise concerns. Additionally, caution should be taken with summaries, headlines, or media interpretations, which can distort the underlying evidence. Accurate information provides a strong, defensible foundation for decision-making.

Purpose: What is the intended goal of the information, and are there signs of bias?
Information produced by relatively neutral parties (like independent researchers or analysts) is preferred. This doesn't mean we dismiss evidence produced by advocates or stakeholders (who have clear interests in a particular outcome),  but we should evaluate it with a more critical lens, especially regarding bias and framing.

 

 

 

Evidence Limitations

Concept Explanation
Correlation is Not Causation Just because two variables are related does not mean one causes the other. A third, unmeasured variable may explain the relationship. Correlational data should be interpreted more cautiously than causal conclusions drawn from true experiments.
Sample Selection Bias When the sample used in a study does NOT represent the population to which results are being applied. This can distort conclusions when findings from one group (e.g., in-person employees) are generalized to a different group (e.g., remote employees). It can also occur in practice when decisions rely on incomplete, skewed, or self-selected data sources, such as only successful cases or vocal stakeholders.
Ecological Fallacy Applying broad trends (e.g., national averages) to specific cases (e.g., a single city or organization) can misrepresent reality by ignoring local or contextual differences such as cost of living, labor markets, or industry norms.
Overgeneralization Drawing conclusions from a narrow or context-specific dataset and applying them too broadly without justification. This occurs when limitations of the original evidence are ignored, such as applying findings from a pilot study or single department to an entire organization.
P-Value vs Effect Size Statistical significance (p-value) indicates whether an observed effect is likely due to chance, but does not indicate the size or practical importance of the effect. Misinterpreting statistical significance as meaningful impact can lead to exaggerated conclusions; effect size is needed to assess real-world relevance.
Lying with Pictures Data visualizations can mislead when designed poorly or manipulated. Common issues include truncated axes, uneven scaling, missing baselines, or selective data presentation. These practices can exaggerate or minimize trends. Ethical visualization requires accurate scaling, clear labeling, and transparency in data presentation.
